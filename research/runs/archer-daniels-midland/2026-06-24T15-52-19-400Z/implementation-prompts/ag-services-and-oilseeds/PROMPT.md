## Company Thesis

- Global crop processing platform
  ADM is a large agricultural supply-chain manager and processor, connecting farmers, crop procurement, transportation, storage, commodity merchandising, and ingredient manufacturing across a global footprint.
  The company reported 2025 revenue of about $80.3 billion, with Ag Services and Oilseeds contributing the majority of external revenue and Carbohydrate Solutions adding a large starches, sweeteners, ethanol, and milling base.
- Physical scale and commodity coordination
  ADM's moat is built from grain origination relationships, hundreds of procurement and processing sites, logistics, risk management, working capital, food-grade manufacturing, and the ability to match crop supply with industrial buyers at scale.
  The business is less protected by consumer brand loyalty than many packaged-foods companies, but it is difficult to replace because commodity handling depends on local elevators, quality grading, bulk transportation, hedging, regulatory compliance, and high-throughput processing assets.

### Moat narrative
- ADM has a strong operational moat in crop origination, oilseed processing, carbohydrate processing, transportation, and risk management. Farmers and industrial buyers value reliability, scale, logistics, credit, hedging, technical support, and food-grade consistency.
- The moat is weaker than a closed software ecosystem because crops, oilseeds, starches, sweeteners, and ethanol are commodity-like products. Still, replacing ADM requires coordinated physical assets, quality systems, financing, and logistics rather than a simple open-source software substitute.

### Decentralization narrative
- ADM is partially decentralizable at the edges: farmer-owned records, cooperative purchasing, local food hubs, regional processors, and transparent commodity exchanges can reduce dependence on centralized origination and distribution channels.
- Full decentralization is difficult because large customers need consistent quality, bulk volume, food-safety controls, price-risk management, freight coordination, and dependable fulfillment. The most credible pressure comes from regional cooperatives, open farm-data tools, local oilseed or grain processing, and open bioprocess design rather than from a single protocol replacing ADM.

## Replacement Sketch

- A realistic replacement path is regional and cooperative rather than a single global clone. Farmers, independent elevators, local processors, haulers, and buyers can coordinate through open records, federated marketplaces, transparent quality data, and member-owned trading channels.
- The first pressure points are crop marketing, local fulfillment, traceability, and selected oilseed or grain processing niches. High-volume global merchandising and industrial crush capacity remain much harder to decentralize.
