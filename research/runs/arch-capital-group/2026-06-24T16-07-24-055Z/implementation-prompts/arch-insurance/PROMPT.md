## Company Thesis

- Business profile
  Arch Capital Group writes specialty property and casualty insurance, reinsurance, and mortgage insurance through global operations. Its platform spans Arch Insurance, Arch Re, and Arch Mortgage, giving the company exposure to primary underwriting, ceded-risk capital, mortgage credit risk, and investment income.
  In 2025, Arch reported $22.9 billion of gross premiums written, $4.36 billion of net income available to common shareholders, $3.7 billion of after-tax operating income, and $26.9 billion of total capitalization. The Insurance segment wrote $7.8 billion of net premiums, the Reinsurance segment wrote $7.6 billion, and the Mortgage segment remained a major profit contributor.
- Registry framing
  For Free The World, Arch is best viewed as an institutional risk-capital and underwriting incumbent rather than a consumer app company. Its moat comes from capital strength, underwriting discipline, claims handling, broker and cedant relationships, ratings, licenses, risk models, and a diversified specialty book.
  The most credible decentralization pressure is modular: open policy administration, transparent catastrophe models, parametric triggers, cooperative risk pools, and capital marketplaces can pressure selected workflows without immediately replacing regulated insurance and reinsurance balance sheets.

### Moat narrative
- Arch has a strong moat because specialty insurance and reinsurance buyers value claims-paying reliability, underwriting judgment, financial strength ratings, broker access, cedant relationships, and capital depth. These advantages are reinforced by Arch's diversified Insurance, Reinsurance, and Mortgage segments and by its long record of disciplined underwriting through market cycles.
- That moat is institutional rather than purely technological. It can be pressured around administration, modelling, and narrow parametric products, but the core balance-sheet, solvency, licensing, claims, and catastrophe-risk functions remain difficult for decentralized or open alternatives to replicate at scale.

### Decentralization narrative
- Arch is only moderately decentralizable in the near term. Insurance workflows can use open-source administration, open catastrophe modelling, auditable data standards, and parametric claims logic, but regulated risk-bearing, capital adequacy, fraud control, claim disputes, and correlated catastrophe losses still require accountable institutions.
- The strongest free-world path is not a full Arch clone. It is a layered ecosystem where affinity groups, mutuals, captives, smaller carriers, and capital providers share open infrastructure for policy workflows, exposure data, risk models, event triggers, and reserve transparency while using licensed carriers or reinsurers where regulation and solvency require them.

## Replacement Sketch

- A credible replacement would start with narrower specialty risks, affinity groups, and administrative workflows rather than broad admitted-carrier substitution. Open policy administration, shared underwriting records, and transparent reserve reporting can reduce dependence on proprietary carrier systems for smaller schemes.
- For observable risks, parametric add-ons can reduce claims friction by paying from predefined triggers, while licensed fronting, reinsurance, or captive structures remain necessary where regulation and solvency requirements apply.
