## Company Thesis

- Off-price retail scale
  TJX Companies is one of the largest off-price retailers in the world, with more than 5,200 stores, six e-commerce sites, and approximately 377,000 associates at fiscal 2025 year end.
  Its U.S. Marmaxx segment includes T.J. Maxx, Marshalls, and Sierra; HomeGoods and Homesense form the U.S. home fashions segment; and TJX Canada and TJX International extend the model through Winners, Marshalls, HomeSense, TK Maxx, and Homesense banners.
- Business model
  TJX's model depends on opportunistic buying, rapid inventory turns, vendor relationships, store-level treasure-hunt merchandising, and broad consumer appeal across income levels. The company says it generally sells comparable merchandise at prices 20% to 60% below full-price retailers' regular prices.
  In fiscal 2025, TJX reported $56.36 billion of net sales and $4.86 billion of net income. Marmaxx generated $34.60 billion of sales, while HomeGoods generated $9.39 billion, making the U.S. apparel and home banners the center of the company's profit engine.

### Moat narrative
- TJX's moat is strong because it is not simply a discount store chain. It is a scaled off-price purchasing and merchandising network that can absorb large volumes of vendor closeouts, seasonal overruns, and brand-name inventory while preserving a store experience that feels opportunistic rather than distressed.
- The company benefits from vendor breadth, buyer expertise, physical-store density, consumer habit, and inventory liquidity. Its value proposition is harder to copy than a website because much of the advantage lives in procurement timing, store operations, and the perceived thrill of finding limited merchandise.

### Decentralization narrative
- TJX is more decentralizable than a hard industrial monopoly, but less vulnerable than a purely digital marketplace. Apparel, accessories, and home goods can flow through resale, repair, local consignment, peer-to-peer markets, and small maker networks, which means the underlying consumer job is not locked to one centralized retailer.
- The hardest part to decentralize is not listing goods online; it is recreating TJX's disciplined buying, authentication, return handling, physical discovery experience, and high-throughput logistics. Decentralized marketplaces, open pattern libraries, and local circular retail networks can pressure portions of demand, but they do not yet match TJX's convenience, assortment density, or consumer trust at national scale.

## Replacement Sketch

- A decentralized HomeGoods alternative would likely combine local resale, repair, refurbished furniture, maker catalogs, and open design files rather than trying to replicate national closeout buying.
- The home category has more room for local fabrication and refurbishment than apparel because furniture, shelving, decor, and repair parts can sometimes be produced, refinished, or customized in local workshops.
