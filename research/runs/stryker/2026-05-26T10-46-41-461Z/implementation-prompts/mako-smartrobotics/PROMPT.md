## Company Thesis

- Business mix
  Stryker operates as a global medical technology company with two reporting segments: MedSurg and Neurotechnology, and Orthopaedics. Its product base spans surgical equipment, navigation, endoscopy, emergency care, neurovascular devices, spinal implants, trauma products, hip implants, knee implants, and robotic-arm assisted orthopedic surgery.
  The company’s 2025 Form 10-K reported $25.116 billion of net sales, with growth supported by procedure demand, capital equipment, implants, and acquisitions. Mako SmartRobotics and Triathlon knees sit inside a broader hospital-facing commercial system that combines regulated hardware, clinical training, surgeon relationships, service, and implant pull-through.
- Registry relevance
  Stryker is a useful Free The World case because the products are physical, highly regulated, and clinically embedded rather than simple software monopolies. That makes near-term displacement difficult, but it also clarifies where open biomedical models, distributed fabrication, transparent robotics platforms, and cooperative clinical purchasing could exert pressure over time.

### Moat narrative
- Stryker’s moat is strong because medical devices compound regulatory clearances, hospital procurement relationships, surgeon training, clinical evidence, field service, proprietary instrument sets, and implant ecosystem lock-in. Mako adds a capital-equipment layer that can reinforce Triathlon and other implant franchises by tying preoperative planning, haptic execution, training, and procedural workflows to Stryker’s system.
- The moat is not absolute. Hospitals are cost-sensitive, governments scrutinize medical spending, and orthopedic robotics is an active competitive field. However, switching from an installed robotic platform or validated implant system is slow because replacement requires clinical confidence, workflow retraining, service support, and regulatory evidence rather than only lower component cost.

### Decentralization narrative
- Stryker’s core products are only moderately decentralizable in the near term. Joint implants and surgical robots must meet demanding safety, sterility, reliability, traceability, cybersecurity, and regulatory requirements, so an open file or low-cost local workshop is not enough to replace a commercial system used in surgery.
- The credible decentralization path is indirect: open biomechanical datasets, open surgical robotics research platforms, shared validation protocols, modular training tools, distributed production of non-implant instruments and anatomical models, and cooperative purchasing networks can reduce information asymmetry and some cost pressure. Actual implant manufacturing remains the hardest layer because quality systems and liability remain centralized constraints.

## Replacement Sketch

- A credible open replacement would not begin as a fully cleared commercial surgical robot. It would start as an open research and simulation stack for planning, navigation, instrument tracking, and procedure rehearsal, then move into validated modules that hospitals can inspect, test, and procure without single-vendor lock-in.
- The practical pressure point is transparency and modularity: open robotics research, interoperable imaging and navigation interfaces, independently validated safety cases, and cooperative hospital purchasing could weaken proprietary platform lock-in even if final clinical deployment remains tightly regulated.
