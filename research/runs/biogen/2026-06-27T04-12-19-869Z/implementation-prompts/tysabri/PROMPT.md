## Company Thesis

- Specialty biotechnology portfolio
  Biogen is a Cambridge, Massachusetts biotechnology company with a commercial portfolio centered on neurological, rare-disease, immunology, and specialty medicines. Its 2025 annual report lists $9.891 billion in total revenue, with growth products including Spinraza, Vumerity, Skyclarys, Qalsody, Zurzuvae, and Biogen's share of Leqembi collaboration economics.
  The seeded products show two important sides of the business: Spinraza is a rare-disease antisense oligonucleotide therapy for spinal muscular atrophy, while Tysabri is a mature multiple sclerosis biologic now facing biosimilar pressure.
- Registry fit
  Biogen belongs in the health-care biotechnology taxonomy because its moat comes from regulated medicine development, clinical evidence, biologic and oligonucleotide manufacturing know-how, payer access, patient support, and specialist-prescriber relationships.
  For Free The World, the relevant decentralization question is not whether patients can safely replace these medicines themselves. It is whether open discovery tools, FDA biosimilar pathways, federated outcomes networks, cooperative purchasing, and public-interest development models can reduce dependence on a small group of sponsor-controlled therapeutic franchises.

### Moat narrative
- Biogen has a meaningful but pressured moat. Spinraza and Tysabri each generated more than $1.5 billion of 2025 revenue, and both depend on regulatory approval, clinical confidence, specialist workflows, reimbursement, pharmacovigilance, and manufacturing quality systems that are hard to replicate casually.
- The moat is weaker than a fresh high-growth platform franchise because Biogen's multiple sclerosis portfolio is declining, Tysabri is exposed to biosimilar competition, and several legacy products face generic or competitive pressure. Biogen's durability therefore depends on execution in newer products, late-stage pipeline assets, and continued management of mature-product erosion.

### Decentralization narrative
- Direct decentralized substitution of Biogen's current medicines is low-readiness. Spinraza requires prescription use, intrathecal delivery into cerebrospinal fluid, ongoing monitoring, and regulated manufacturing; Tysabri requires infusion workflows, safety monitoring, and risk controls for progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy.
- Credible decentralization pressure is strongest around the market structure surrounding the drugs: open target-discovery infrastructure, federated real-world evidence, transparent safety registries, regulated biosimilar entry, and cooperative buyer networks. These mechanisms can pressure pricing and information control without pretending that unregulated local drug production is appropriate.

## Replacement Sketch

- A plausible replacement path runs through regulated biosimilars, transparent safety monitoring, and buyer coordination rather than an informal monoclonal-antibody copy.
- Open and federated infrastructure can matter by helping clinics, payers, and patient communities compare outcomes, infusion burden, safety risk, and net cost across Tysabri, biosimilars, and other multiple sclerosis therapies.
