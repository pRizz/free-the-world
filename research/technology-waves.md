# Technology waves used in scoring

This note exists so the writing and scoring model does not quietly assume a static world.

## Core leaning

The registry should evaluate companies as if the following trends keep improving materially over the next decade:

- AI keeps compressing expertise and operational coordination costs.
- Open-source software keeps reducing the premium that incumbents can charge for generic workflow layers.
- Bitcoin and Lightning make micropayments, spam-resistance, and protocol-native incentives easier to build.
- Distributed manufacturing keeps reducing the minimum useful size of a factory.
- Energy generation and management become more modular, local, and software-defined.

## Specific waves

### 1. 3D plastic printing
- Reduces friction for replacement parts, enclosures, and niche manufacturing.
- Weakens long-tail hardware and spare-parts rent capture.

### 2. 3D metal printing
- Matters more slowly, but shifts serious manufacturing capabilities closer to smaller operators.
- Makes some industrial supply chains less sacred over long arcs.

### 3. PCB and chip-adjacent printing
- Not a magic wand for frontier fabs, but a real pressure term for electronics prototyping and localized assembly.
- Makes it easier to imagine more modular hardware ecosystems.

### 4. Pick-and-place automation
- Shrinks the labor and coordination burden for localized electronics production.
- Helps turn small-batch manufacturing from artisanal chaos into repeatable workflow.

### 5. Printable solar and localized wind
- Makes energy stacks more distributed and less vendor-totalizing.
- Raises the value of open control layers and interoperable energy software.

### 6. Microfactories and automated mini-home factories
- Suggests a world where some manufacturing and fulfillment look more local, modular, and software-defined.
- Particularly relevant to retail, consumer hardware, spare parts, and housing-adjacent systems.

## How to use this note

- Treat these waves as strategic context, not present-day fact inflation.
- Distinguish between **current evidence** and **future pressure**.
- Use them to shape moat and decentralizability reasoning, especially where products appear durable only because the old production assumptions still dominate.
